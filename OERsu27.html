1.5.3.3 Disjoint union of sets

Given any set \(a\) and a family \(\bigl ({\mathcal {s}}_{x}\bigr )_{{x} \in {a}}\) indexed by \(a\), let: \begin {equation*} \sum _{x \in a}\mathcal {s}_x = \bigcup _{x \in a}\left (\mathcal {s}_x \times \{x\}\right ). \end {equation*}

Thus: \(z \in \sum _{x \in a}\mathcal {s}_x \Leftrightarrow (\exists x \in a)(\exists z' \in \mathcal {s}_x)(z = \opair {z'}{x})\).

Some examples first:

i.
Evidently, \(\sum _{x \in \emptyset }\mathcal {s}_x = \emptyset \).
ii.
If \(\bigl ({\mathcal {s}}_{x}\bigr )_{{x} \in {a}}\) is a family containing the empty set (i.e., \((\exists x \in a)(\mathcal {s}_x = \emptyset )\)) then \(\sum _{x \in a}\mathcal {s}_x = \sum _{x \in a'}\mathcal {s'}_x\), where \(a' = a \setminus \finv {\mathcal {s}}{\emptyset } = \left \{x \in a: \mathcal {s}_x \neq \emptyset \right \}\) and \(\mathcal {s'} = \rest {\mathcal {s}}{a'}\). Hence it is customary to consider families which does not contain the empty set.
iii.
If \(a = 3\) then taking the family \(\mathcal {s}_i = i + 1\) (\(i = 0, 1, 2\)), it is easy to see that: \begin {equation*} \sum _{i \in 3}\mathcal {s}_i = \left \{\opair {0}{0}, \opair {0}{1}, \opair {0}{2}, \opair {1}{1}, \opair {1}{2}, \opair {2}{2}\right \}; \end {equation*} on the other hand \(\bigcup _{i \in 3}\mathcal {s}_i = 3 = \{0, 1, 2\}\). Observe: \(\sum _{i \in 3}\mathcal {s}_i\) contains \(0\) three times since in the family it occurs three times, once in each \(1, 2, 3\).

Thus it seems that \(\sum _{x \in a}\mathcal {s}_x\) is producing a set in which each element \(t \in \mathcal {s}_x\) appear as many times as it is contained in elements \(x \in a\), and in each occurence it shows from which \(x \in a\) it did come from. This motivates the name for \(\sum _{x \in a}\mathcal {s}_x\) as the disjoint union of sets \(\mathcal {s}_x\), \(x \in a\) as opposed to the union \(\bigcup _{x \in a}\mathcal {s}_x\) of sets \(\mathcal {s}_x\) for \(x \in a\) — see Figure 1.13 for a schematic picture depicting the difference. Evidently, if \(\bigl ({\mathcal {s}}_{x}\bigr )_{{x} \in {a}}\) is mutually exclusive then \(\bigcup _{x \in a}\mathcal {s}_x\) and \(\sum _{x \in a}\mathcal {s}_x\) are in bijective correspondence (provide a bijection).

Theorem. Given any small set \(a\) and a family \(\bigl ({\mathcal {s}}_{x}\bigr )_{{x} \in {a}}\), there are functions \(\mathcal {s}_x \xrightarrow {i_x} \sum _{x \in a}\mathcal {s}_x\) (\(x \in a\)) defined by \(i_x(t) = \opair {t}{x}\) with the property: if there is a set \(k\) and for each \(x \in a\) there is a function \(\mathcal {s}_x \xrightarrow {f_x} k\) then there exists a unique function \(\sum _{x \in a}\mathcal {s}_x \xrightarrow {f} k\) such that \(f_x = \comp {f}{i_x}\) (\(x \in a\)).

Assume: \(T\) is a set and for each \(x \in a\) there exist functions \(\mathcal {s}_x \xrightarrow {i'_x} T\) with the property: for any set \(k\) and functions \(\mathcal {s}_x \xrightarrow {f_x} k\) for each \(x \in a\) there exists a unique function \(T \xrightarrow {f} k\) such that \(f_x = \comp {f}{i'_x}\) (\(x \in a\)). Then there exists a unique bijection \(T \xrightarrow {p} \sum _{x \in a}\mathcal {s}_x\) such that \(i_x = \comp {p}{i'_x}\) (\(x \in a\)). ///

Proof. For the first part, show that the assignment \(\opair {t}{x} \mapsto f_x(t)\) for \(t \in x \in a\) suffices for \(f\). The second part immediately follows from the first part; complete the details. □

Remark 1.69. The statement in Theorem can be phrased in terms of the diagrams: \begin {equation*} \xymatrix { {\mathcal {s}_x} \ar [r]^-{i_x} \ar [dr]_-{f_x} & {\sum _{x \in a}\mathcal {s}_x} \ar @[red]@{.>}[d]^-{\color {red}!\, f} \\ & {k} } \end {equation*} for each \(x \in a\). Observe: the diagram looks almost like the diagram of Remark 1.65 with directions of arrows reversed!

If \(a = 2\) and \(\bigl ({\mathcal {s}}_{x}\bigr )_{{x} \in {2}}\) is the unordered pair \(\left \{\mathcal {s}_0, \mathcal {s}_1\right \}\) and then \begin {equation*} \sum _{x \in 2}\mathcal {s}_x = \left \{u: (\exists t \in \mathcal {s}_0)(u = \opair {t}{0}) \LOR (\exists s \in \mathcal {s}_1)(u = \opair {s}{1})\right \} \end {equation*} is the disjoint union of \(\mathcal {s}_0, \mathcal {s}_1\), the functions \(i_0, i_1\) are \(t \overset {i_0}{\longmapsto } \opair {t}{0}\), \(s \overset {i_1}{\longmapsto } \opair {s}{1}\), while given functions \(\xymatrix { {\mathcal {s}_0} \ar [r]^-{f_0} & {k} \ar @{<-}[r]^-{f_1} & {\mathcal {s}_1} }\) the unique function \(f\) ensured by Theorem is \(f(u) = \begin {cases} f_0(t), & \text { if }u = \opair {t}{0} \\ f_1(s), & \text { if }u = \opair {s}{1} \end {cases}\). Thus \(f\) pastes together the functions \(f_0, f_1\), producing the commutative diagram: \begin {equation*} \xymatrix { {\mathcal {s}_0} \ar [r]^-{i_0} \ar [dr]_-{f_0} & {\sum _{x \in 2}\mathcal {s}_x} \ar @{<-}[r]^-{i_1} \ar @[red]@{.>}[d]|-{\color {red}!\, f} & {\mathcal {s}_1} \ar [dl]^-{f_1} \\ & {k} }; \end {equation*} compare with the diagram in Remark 1.30, as if replaced with the direction of arrows reversed — making way for another name for the set \(\sum _{x \in 2}\mathcal {s}_x\) as the coproduct of \(\mathcal {s}_0, \mathcal {s}_1\) and henceforth written as \(\mathcal {s}_0 + \mathcal {s}_1\). The functions \(i_0, i_1\) are called coproduct injections (justify they are indeed monomorphisms) and the unique function \(f\) by \(\begin {bmatrix} {f_0} \\ {f_1} \end {bmatrix}\).

In general, \(n \geq 1\) is a natural number, \(x_1, x_2, \dots , x_n\) are small sets then their disjoint union is denoted by \(\sum _{1 \leq i \leq n}x_i\), the functions \(i_{x_i}\) are called coproduct injections and for functions \(x_i \xrightarrow {f_i} k\) (\(1 \leq i \leq n\)) the unique function \(f\) is denoted by \( \begin {bmatrix} f_1 \\ f_2 \\ \dots \\ f_n \end {bmatrix}\).

More generally, given a family \(\bigl ({\mathcal {s}}_{x}\bigr )_{{x} \in {a}}\) of sets indexed by a set \(a\), its coproduct is \(\sum _{x\in a}\mathcal {s}_x\) along with the coproduct injections \(\mathcal {s}_x \xrightarrow {i_x} \sum _{x \in a}\mathcal {s}_x\) and the unique function \(\sum _{x \in a}\mathcal {s}_x \xrightarrow {f} k\) existing for the functions \(\mathcal {s}_x \xrightarrow {f_x} k\) being denoted by \([f_x]_{x \in a}\).

Another use of Theorem in proving coproducts:

Lemma. For any \(n, m \in \Bb {N}\), the coproduct of \(n, m\) is the sum \(n + m\) of the natural numbers. ///

Proof. Show that \(\xymatrix { {n} \ar [r]^-{i_n} & {n + m} \ar @{<-}[r]^-{i_m} & {m} }\) defined by \(i_n(x) = x, 0 \leq x < n\) and \(i_m(x) = n + x, 0 \leq x < m\) satisfies the second part of Theorem. □

Proposition. Given the morphism \(\seq {\mathcal {r}}{x}{a} \xrightarrow {\seq {f}{x}{a}} \seq {\mathcal {s}}{x}{a}\) of families indexed by a set \(a\), there exists a unique function \(w\) making the diagrams: \begin {equation*} \xymatrix { {\mathcal {r}_x} \ar [r]^-{i_x} \ar [d]_-{f_x} & {\sum _{x \in a}\mathcal {r}_x} \ar @[red]@{.>}[d]^-{\color {red}!\,w} \\ {\mathcal {s}_x} \ar [r]_-{i_x} & {\sum _{x \in a}\mathcal {s}_x} } \end {equation*} to commute for each \(x \in a\). ///

Proof. Use Theorem and complete the details. □

Remark 1.70. Observe: \begin {equation*} w\opair {t}{x} = \opair {f_x(t)}{x}, \quad \text { for }t \in \mathcal {s}_x, x \in a. \end {equation*} Observe: the set for \(w\) and the set \(\sum _{x \in a}f_x\) are in bijective correspondence natural in the families \(\bigl ({\mathcal {s}}_{x}\bigr )_{{x} \in {a}}\) and \(\bigl ({\mathcal {t}}_{x}\bigr )_{{x} \in {a}}\) (justify). Hence, in line with the agreement made in Remark 1.31 and Remark 1.66, the function \(w\) could as well be represented by \(\sum _{x \in a}f_x\) yielding: \begin {equation*} \left (\sum _{x \in a}f_x\right )\opair {t}{x} = \opair {f_x(t)}{x}, \quad \text { for }t \in \mathcal {s}_x, x \in a. \end {equation*}

Corollary. Given small sets \(a, k\) and a family \(\bigl ({\mathcal {s}}_{x}\bigr )_{{x} \in {a}}\) indexed by \(a\), there exists a bijection \(\Homo {\Set }{\sum _{x \in a}\mathcal {s}_x}{k} \xrightarrow {\Phi _{k, \mathcal {s}}} \prod _{x \in a}\Homo {\Set }{\mathcal {s}_x}{k}\) defined by \begin {equation*} \left (\Arr {h}{\sum _{x \in a}\mathcal {s}_x}{k}\right ) \overset {\Phi _{k,\mathcal {s}}}{\longmapsto } \left (\comp {h}{i_x}\right )_{x \in a}, \end {equation*} natural in \(k\) and the family \(\bigl ({\mathcal {s}}_{x}\bigr )_{{x} \in {a}}\). ///

Proof. Show that the diagram: \begin {equation*} \xymatrix { {\Homo {\Set }{\sum _{x \in a}\mathcal {s}_x}{k}} \ar @{>->>}[]!<2.4ex,0ex>;[r]^-{\Phi _{k, \mathcal {s}}} \ar [d]_-{\comp {u}{\comp {-}{\left (\sum _{x \in a}f_x\right )}}} & {\prod _{x \in a}\Homo {\Set }{\mathcal {s}_x}{k}} \ar [d]^-{\left (\comp {u}{\comp {-}{f_x}}\right )_{x \in a}} \\ {\Homo {\Set }{\sum _{x \in a}\mathcal {r}_x}{l}} \ar @{>->>}[]!<2.4ex,0ex>;[r]^-{\Phi _{l, \mathcal {r}}} & {\prod _{x \in a}\Homo {\Set }{\mathcal {r}_x}{l}} } \end {equation*} commutes for each function \(k \xrightarrow {u} l\) and morphism \(\seq {\mathcal {r}}{x}{a} \xrightarrow {\seq {f}{x}{a}} \seq {\mathcal {s}}{x}{a}\) of families. □

1.5.3.3.1 Copower of sets Given small sets \(a, b\), consider the family \(\bigl ({\mathcal {b}}_{x}\bigr )_{{x} \in {a}}\) where \(\mathcal {b}_x = b\) for each \(x \in a\). The coproduct \(\sum _{x \in a}\mathcal {b}_x\) is often called the \(a\)-fold copower of sets; observe, from definition: \begin {align*} \sum _{x \in a}\mathcal {b}_x & = \bigcup _{x \in a}(\mathcal {b}_x \times \{x\}) \\ & = b \times a. \end {align*}

The coproduct injections are denoted by \(b \xrightarrow {i^a_x} \sum _{x \in a}\mathcal {b}_x\) (\(x \in a\)), which has the property: for any family \(\left (\Arr {f_x}{b}{k}\right )_{x \in a}\) of functions indexed by \(a\), there exists the unique function \(\sum _{x\in a}\mathcal {b}_x \xrightarrow {\left [f_x\right ]_{x \in a}} k\) such that \(f_x = \comp {\left [f_x\right ]_{x \in a}}{i^a_x}\) for each \(x \in a\) (Theorem 1.5.3.2).
